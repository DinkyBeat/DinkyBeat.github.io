SENIOR ASSASSIN OPERATIONAL PLAYBOOK

Classification: Eyes Only. Never read aloud. Never screenshot and share. Memorize and destroy if necessary.

SECTION 0 — OPERATING PRINCIPLES

Principle 1: Information Asymmetry Wins Games. You do not win by being faster or braver. You win by knowing more than your target and your hunter at every moment. Every tactic in this document is downstream of this principle.

Principle 2: Minimize Exposure. Every second you spend in an assassination zone without executing is a liability. Move with purpose. Don't linger. Don't improvise in the field — improvisation means your pre-op planning failed.

Principle 3: Asymmetric Discomfort. You are willing to wake at 5AM in the cold and wait 90 minutes in a parked car. Most targets are not. This willingness is your primary competitive advantage. Exploit it systematically.

Principle 4: Operational Security (OPSEC) Is Absolute. One leak ends your game. Not probably. Certainly. Treat every senior as a potential information conduit to your hunter. Speak about the game to no one except your partner, and only through your designated secure channel.

Principle 5: Pre-Plan Everything, Decide Nothing in the Field. Decisions made under stress and time pressure are bad decisions. Every contingency in this document exists so that when something goes wrong, you execute a pre-built response, not a panicked improvisation.

SECTION 1 — PRE-GAME INTELLIGENCE OPERATIONS (Before April 13th)

You have a window before the game starts where targets are not yet assigned and no one is paranoid. This is your highest-value intelligence collection period. Use every hour of it.

1.1 — Target Dossier Construction

Build a dossier on every likely target before targets drop on April 12th at 8PM. When your target is revealed, you want to already have their profile — not be starting intelligence collection from zero while they're already on defense.

For each potential target, collect and record the following:

Residential Profile:

  • Full home address
  • Google Maps satellite view — screenshot and annotate: all entry/exit points, garage type (attached/detached, automatic/manual), driveway orientation, sight lines from street, nearest cross streets, available concealment positions (neighboring yards, parked cars, tree lines, fences)
  • Exterior lighting — is the driveway lit at night? Motion-sensor lights?
  • Neighbor activity levels — busy street or quiet? Dogs?
  • Parking situation — do they park in the garage, in the driveway, or on the street?
  • Distance and drive time from your home (critical for purge response)

Vehicle Profile:

  • Make, model, color
  • License plate if obtainable
  • Any distinguishing features

Schedule Profile:

  • Morning departure window for school (cross-reference Splash cadence data)
  • Work schedule if employed — days, hours, location
  • Sports or practice schedule — sport, days, times, facility location
  • Regular non-home locations: gym, friend's houses, recurring hangout spots
  • After-school routine — do they go straight home, stop somewhere, go to practice?

Social Profile:

  • Closest friends — who do they move with?
  • Who in their social circle is also in the game? (potential information leak nodes)
  • Splash username — begin cadence mapping immediately (see Section 1.2)

1.2 — Splash Cadence Mapping

Splash updates every 10 minutes. This means you can build a movement profile on any target by logging their location at each update interval over multiple days. Start now, before they're paranoid and managing their broadcast.

What to log: Timestamp, location name or description, whether they appear stationary or moving. Do this for at least 3 consecutive days to identify patterns versus anomalies.

What you're building: A time-tagged movement map. You want to know where they are at 6AM, 7AM, 3PM, 6PM, 10PM. Consistency across days reveals exploitable routine. Variance tells you how unpredictable they are.

The Proxy Observer: Your cleanest long-term intel asset. Recruit a trusted non-participant — someone not in the game — and have them add your targets on Splash. They monitor and relay locations to you. Your Splash account never appears in their contacts. They have no reason to manage their broadcast around a non-participant. This gives you persistent invisible surveillance.

What to look for:

  • Consistent morning departure window — note the time bracket and how tight the variance is
  • Regular non-home evening locations — these are secondary strike opportunities
  • Location gaps — when does their dot go dark? Phone dead, or location disabled? Note exact times. A target who regularly disables location at 9PM and re-enables at 11PM has a pattern you can work around.
  • Unusual pings near your own house — this is how you identify who is hunting you

1.3 — Purge Response Pre-Planning

Per the rules, purges are called with 20 minutes warning. Targets must leave their house and be outside for a minimum of 10 minutes. This is a mandatory exposure window that you know about in advance.

Before the game starts, build and memorize a purge response matrix:

  • Drive time from your home to each potential target's home (calculate now, during low-traffic hours matching likely purge times)
  • Designated parking position at each target's home (from your satellite recon)
  • Approach route on foot from parking position to target's likely exit point
  • Who drives, who executes, who films — pre-assigned, non-negotiable

The teams that win purge rounds move in the first 3 minutes of the 20-minute window. Teams that lose spend 8 minutes deliberating and arrive late. The matrix eliminates deliberation.

1.4 — Equipment Procurement

Water gun: Acquire the longest-range reliable water gun available. Long range means you do not need to close to within arm's reach to execute. Distance is safety and flexibility. Confirm it has or can have a colored tip per rules.

Per-person loadout for any operation:

  • Water gun, fully loaded, colored tip confirmed
  • Phone, fully charged
  • Car charger
  • Food and water for up to 2 hours of stationary wait
  • Dark, non-distinctive clothing — nothing memorable, nothing bright
  • Physical warmth layer if operating before 7AM

SECTION 2 — OFFENSIVE OPERATIONS

2.1 — Operation Framework

Every offensive operation has four phases: Intelligence, Staging, Execution, Exfil. You do not proceed to the next phase until the current phase is complete. Skipping phases is how you get burned.

Intelligence: What do you know about the target's current location, schedule, and awareness state? Is your hunter also out in the field right now? Is it safe for you to be mobile?

Staging: Position yourself and your partner optimally before the target is aware of your presence. This includes your Splash cover (bait ping or going dark), your parking position, your approach route, your communication protocol.

Execution: The hit itself. Clean, documented, unambiguous. One continuous video, colored tip visible, clean soak, target reaction captured.

Exfil: Leave immediately after the hit. Do not linger. Do not celebrate on site. Clear the area within 60 seconds of execution.

2.2 — Primary Strike Window: Early Morning Stakeout

The morning departure is your highest-percentage offensive opportunity. Targets are operating on a fixed schedule (school start time is immovable), are mentally in morning autopilot, and have limited time to wait you out. The destination is protected (school property) but the departure is not.

T-minus 24 hours — Reconnaissance Drive:

  • Drive past the target's house once, casually, at a similar time of day to when you'll be operating
  • Confirm: garage door orientation, available parking positions, sight lines, neighbor activity
  • Do not slow down. Do not stop. One pass.
  • Note whether any exterior lights are motion-triggered

T-minus 12 hours — Confirmation Check:

  • Confirm via Splash that target is home
  • Check your own Splash — is your hunter near your house? If yes, assess whether it's safe to leave in the morning
  • Final equipment check
  • Sleep. Fatigue causes execution errors.

T-minus 20 minutes — Bait Ping Execution:

  • Go to a location approximately 1 mile from the target's house in a direction away from your actual approach route
  • Wait for your Splash location to register at that position (confirm with your proxy observer if needed)
  • Leave immediately after the ping registers
  • Effect: your hunter sees you stationary away from your house and away from the target. You have a 10-minute clean movement window before your next ping updates.
  • Proceed directly to your staging position. You must be parked and in position before the next Splash update.

On-Site Staging:

  • Arrive by 5:30AM or 45 minutes before their expected departure window, whichever is earlier
  • Park 2-3 houses down on the same side of the street as the target's house. Never directly opposite — it creates an obvious observation angle and is more visible to neighbors.
  • Face the car toward your exit route. Engine off.
  • Partner 1 (Executor): Moves to concealed forward position on foot. Ideal position is near the end of the target's driveway, behind a neighboring vehicle, fence, or yard element. Must have direct sight line to target's exit point and be within 15 feet of the driveway end.
  • Partner 2 (Filmmaker/Driver): Remains in the car. Phone in hand, camera app open, filming mode ready. Does not exit the vehicle unless target is confirmed eliminated.
  • Establish live phone call between partners. Do not hang up until exfil is complete. Agree on call words before leaving: "Green" = target visible and moving toward exit. "Execute" = move now. "Abort" = stand down immediately, do not approach.

The Garage Problem — Tactical Solution:

A target who parks in the garage is not invulnerable. They must execute the following sequence every morning without exception:

  1. Garage door opens (automatic: ~15 seconds. Manual: ~30 seconds.)
  2. Target enters vehicle
  3. Car backs out of garage
  4. Car pauses at the end of the driveway to check traffic before pulling into the street
  5. Car departs

Step 4 is your kill window. The car cannot enter a street from a residential driveway without pausing. This pause is physically mandatory. If Partner 1 is already positioned at the end of the driveway before the garage door opens, they are at the car window before the target is moving. The target has no retreat space (car is already out of the garage), no speed (stationary), and no warning (they're looking at traffic, not the driveway end).

Contingency — Garage door opens but target does not emerge within 10 minutes: They may have seen you or gotten a tip. Do not approach the house. Partner 1 returns to the car. Wait 20 more minutes. If still no movement, abort and reassess. Do not blow your cover position for a target who is aware.

Contingency — Target exits but has an escort (parent, sibling, friend): Do not abort automatically. Assess: is the escort a game participant? Witnesses complicate execution but don't prevent it. If the escort is a non-participant, execute normally — they cannot interfere per game rules. If the escort is another game participant who could attempt a counter-hit, abort and wait for an unescorted window.

Contingency — Target sees Partner 1 and retreats inside: Do not pursue to the door. Do not approach the house. Partner 1 walks casually back to the car as if they were a neighbor or pedestrian. Abort the operation. You have now burned your cover position at this location — do not return to the same spot for at least 48 hours.

Contingency — Target exits through a different door than expected: Partner 2 in the car should have visibility on the full property front. If target exits from a side door or unexpected point, Partner 2 calls "Green, side exit" and Partner 1 repositions. Long-range water gun allows engagement from distance if close approach is no longer viable.

2.3 — Secondary Strike Window: Afternoon Return

The after-school return is your second-best window and in some ways easier than mornings: you know exactly where they're coming from, you know the approximate time, and they're mentally decompressed and off-guard.

Setup:

  • Monitor Splash from 2:15PM onward
  • When target's location begins moving away from school vicinity, calculate your drive time to their house. You need to be positioned there before they arrive.
  • In most cases you will have 8-15 minutes of lead time. Use it.
  • Execute bait ping before departing if your hunter is active

Execution: Same staging protocol as morning. Partner 1 forward, Partner 2 filming from the car. The critical difference is the parked car window: per your rules, a parked car with windows down is fair game. Many targets sit in their driveway after arriving home — finishing a call, a song, a text. If they park outside and you are already positioned, this is your cleanest opportunity. Long-range gun means you don't need them to exit the vehicle.

Contingency — Target drives directly into the garage without stopping: Shift to the foot approach. They still have to walk from the car to the door inside the garage, and the garage door takes time to close. If Partner 1 is moving the moment the car turns into the driveway, they may be able to reach the garage entrance before it closes. This is a high-risk approach — assess in real time based on garage door speed and Partner 1's position.

Contingency — Target arrives home with others and goes directly inside: Abort. Do not attempt an execution in a group arrival scenario unless the conditions are extremely clean. Reset and plan for the next window.

2.4 — Tertiary Strike Window: Transitions Between Known Locations

Any time your target moves between two locations where one or both ends are unprotected, you have a strike opportunity. The highest-value transitions are:

  • Work exit: Explicitly listed as fair game. If you know their shift schedule, you know exactly when they'll exit a specific door at a specific location. This is more predictable than their home departure.
  • Gym exit: Explicitly fair game. Same logic — fixed location, predictable timing window.
  • Practice/game exit to their car: They walk from a protected zone (the practice) to an unprotected zone (the parking lot). The parking lot is your territory.
  • Social location exit: If Splash shows them stationary at a friend's house for 2+ hours, they will exit eventually. Position outside and wait.

The "Skip School and Wait" Play: As noted in your original doc — if a target has no work or sports and their home departure is well-defended (garage, alert, varies timing), consider skipping school one day to stake their house for the afternoon return. You have all day. They have to come home eventually. This is an extreme discomfort play that almost no target will have a defense for.

2.5 — The Purge Strike

This is your single highest expected-value opportunity in the entire game. Treat every purge notification as a priority scramble.

The moment the purge notification hits:

  1. Both partners move to the car immediately — no discussion, no deliberation
  2. Driver executes the pre-planned route to the target's home (you built this in Section 1.4)
  3. Execute bait ping en route if time permits
  4. Arrive and stage before or as target is being forced outside
  5. Target must be outside for 10 minutes minimum and cannot re-enter. This is a mandatory, time-bounded exposure window. You know the location. You know the duration. Execute.

Contingency — Target is already outside and mobile when you arrive: Do not chase by car per rules. Park, Partner 1 exits and moves on foot. Long-range gun extends your effective engagement distance significantly.

Contingency — Target goes to a crowded public location during the purge: This is their best defensive move. If they're in a parking lot of a shopping center surrounded by people, your execution becomes logistically harder but not impossible. Assess: can Partner 1 approach cleanly from one direction while Partner 2 creates a distraction or films from distance? If not clean, shadow them until they separate from the crowd.

Contingency — You cannot reach the target in time: Use the purge differently. Go dark on Splash and move freely for the full hour. Conduct reconnaissance on the target's home without risk of being seen as suspicious — everyone is supposed to be out. Note new details about their property, their neighborhood, their patterns during the purge.

2.6 — The Mutual Target Chain (Alliance Operations)

Each round, someone is hunting your target. That team has perfectly aligned interests with you for exactly one week. Contact them.

What you offer: Your Splash cadence data on the target, your intelligence on their home layout and schedule, coordination on timing.

What you receive: Their separate intelligence stream on the same target, a potential coordinated two-vector approach that the target cannot defend against simultaneously.

The two-vector approach: Team A approaches from the front. Team B approaches from a different angle. Target can only run in one direction. One team executes, the other films or provides backup.

Risk: You are sharing intelligence with another team that will eventually be your opponent. Share target-specific information only. Do not reveal your own defensive setup, your home layout, or your schedule. The alliance expires the moment the target is eliminated.

Contingency — Allied team defects and attempts to eliminate you during the operation: This is a real risk in later rounds. Assess the trustworthiness of any ally before sharing sensitive information. In early rounds, defection risk is low — you're not a valuable enough target. In late rounds, treat all alliances with extreme caution.

SECTION 3 — DEFENSIVE OPERATIONS

3.1 — Hunter Identification

Before you can defend effectively, you need to know who has you. Begin identification immediately on April 13th.

Methods:

  • Process of elimination: You know roughly how many teams are in the game. You know some teams' targets from word of mouth. Eliminate teams with known targets. The remainder are candidates for your hunter.
  • Splash surveillance: Any account that appears near your home address more than once with no obvious reason is a candidate. Flag them immediately.
  • Social behavior changes: Has someone who doesn't normally interact with you started being unusually friendly? Has someone who is normally around you become notably absent? Both are signals.
  • Mutual friends: Someone always knows something. You don't have to ask directly. "Who do you think has me?" asked casually to a trusted friend often produces useful intelligence.

Once identified: You now have an intelligence target in addition to your offensive target. Begin Splash cadence mapping on your hunter. Know when they're mobile. Know when they're near your house. This is your early warning system.

3.2 — Daily Departure Protocol

This protocol is executed every single morning without exception from April 13th until you are either eliminated or the game ends.

Step 1 — Pre-departure Splash check: Before doing anything else, check Splash. Is your hunter near your house? Is any unknown account in your neighborhood? If yes, do not depart on your normal schedule. Delay 20-30 minutes and recheck.

Step 2 — Partner sweep: Text your partner or a trusted non-participant to do a slow drive past your street and report back. "Clear" means you proceed. Anything suspicious means you wait for a second sweep or seek an alternate exit.

Step 3 — Vary your timing: Never depart at the same time two days in a row. Vary by at least 15-20 minutes. A hunter who has staked your house based on your consistent 7:10AM departure will wait in the wrong window if you leave at 6:50 or 7:30.

Step 4 — Vary your exit: Front door, garage (if available), side door, back door — rotate. A hunter who has positioned for your front door exit gets nothing if you exit elsewhere.

Step 5 — Equipment: Water gun on your person from the moment you leave your door to the moment you enter a safe zone. If you are eliminated for being unarmed, that is a preparation failure, not bad luck.

Step 6 — Move with purpose: From door to car is your maximum vulnerability window. Have your keys out before you open the door. Do not check your phone. Do not pause. Straight line from exit to vehicle.

3.3 — The Car-to-Door Problem (Afternoon Return)

Per your rules, a parked car with windows down is fair game. The transition from your car to your front door is your second highest vulnerability window of the day.

Protocol:

  • Before turning onto your street, check Splash. Any unknown accounts in your vicinity?
  • Do one slow drive past your house before pulling into your driveway. Scan for parked cars containing occupants, anyone on foot near your property, anything that doesn't fit the normal neighborhood pattern.
  • If anything looks wrong, do not stop. Drive past. Circle the block. Reassess.
  • If clear: park as close to your front door as possible. Keys in hand before the car stops. Exit the car and move directly to the door. Do not sit in the parked car. Do not check your phone in the driveway. Windows up at all times when parked.
  • Have your partner on a live call — they can watch from a window inside and confirm clear before you exit the car.

3.4 — Splash Discipline

Operational rules:

  • Go dark (disable location sharing) at least 15 minutes before any offensive operation departure
  • Never have your live location broadcasting when you are en route to or near a target's home
  • Never post real-time location on any platform during the game — this includes Instagram stories, Snapchat, BeReal, anything

The Bait Ping (Defensive Use): When you need to move through an exposed area or make an unpredictable transit, briefly appear on Splash at a location away from your actual route, then go dark immediately after the ping registers. Your hunter scrambles to a false position. You move freely.

Cadence awareness: Know the 10-minute update cycle. If you need to be somewhere that would reveal your true position on Splash, time your movements so you depart and arrive in the gap between pings. Appear at a neutral location, go dark, move, reappear at a different neutral location.

3.5 — Purge Defense Protocol

You receive 20 minutes warning. You must be outside for the full hour and physically out of your car for at least 10 minutes. Your hunter has the same warning and the same information about where you live.

Defensive purge protocol:

  • Pre-select a defensive purge location now, before the game starts. Ideal characteristics: high foot traffic, multiple entry and exit points, public enough that a water gun hit is socially conspicuous, large enough that you can keep moving
  • Go directly to this location upon receiving the purge notice. Do not mill around your neighborhood.
  • Stay mobile during the 10-minute mandatory outdoor window. A moving target is exponentially harder to execute on than a stationary one.
  • Use the bait ping to show your location at a different public spot before going to your actual defensive location
  • Your partner stays with you. Two sets of eyes scanning approaches.
  • Water gun in hand or immediately accessible at all times during the purge

The Hour of Immunity Rule — Exploit It: Per your rules, if you know who has you and you successfully hit them before they hit you, you receive 1 hour of immunity from that person. If you have confidently identified your hunter, a purge is an opportunity to flip the dynamic — you know they're mobile and exposed, you know roughly where they're going (toward you), and you can attempt to intercept them in transit. If successful, you neutralize the immediate threat and gain an hour of clean offensive operation time.

Contingency — Hunter is already outside your house when the purge hits: Do not exit your front door. Your work exemption requires you to actually be at work, but if you have no legitimate exemption, you must exit. Use an alternate exit — side door, back door — and immediately enter your car and drive away before they can reposition. Do not stop in your driveway. Do not give them the stationary car window.

SECTION 4 — OPERATIONAL SECURITY (OPSEC)

4.1 — Information Compartmentalization

The single most common way teams get eliminated is an information leak through their social network. You cannot control what your hunter knows about you. You can control what information you emit.

Rules:

  • Never speak the name of your target out loud. Not at school, not at home, not in a car. Voices carry. Walls are thin. People overhear.
  • Never discuss strategy in public or semi-public settings
  • All game communication happens through one private channel with your partner only. No group chats. No voice notes to friends. One channel.
  • If asked who your target is, the answer is always: "I can't say." Not a deflection, not a joke — just that phrase, every time, to everyone, including close friends.
  • If asked about your strategy, you have none. You're just playing casually.
  • Treat every senior as a potential information conduit to your hunter. This is not paranoia — it is accurate. Senior classes are dense social networks. Information moves in two hops.

4.2 — Counter-Intelligence

Your hunter is running the same playbook against you that you are running against your target. Anticipate their moves.

The Proxy Observer Defense: You cannot know if a non-participant has been recruited to monitor your Splash. Assume they have. Operate as if your Splash is always being watched by a hostile observer.

Social Engineering Defense: If someone you don't normally talk to suddenly initiates friendly conversation in a public setting, maintain situational awareness. Keep your water gun accessible. Do not become cognitively absorbed in a conversation with someone who may be attempting to distract you for an execution.

SECTION 5 — ROUND-BY-ROUND STRATEGIC ADAPTATION

Round 1 (April 13-18)

Environment: Maximum field size. Most targets are not yet paranoid. Routines are normal. Intelligence collected pre-game is maximally accurate because targets haven't started varying their behavior yet.

Strategic directive: Strike fast. Do not let Round 1 drag to the deadline. Early elimination means you advance with momentum while others are still figuring out the game. Your pre-game intelligence package gives you a decisive advantage in this round that degrades as the game progresses and targets get more careful.

Priority order: Morning stakeout using departure window from Splash cadence mapping. If that fails twice, shift to afternoon return. If that fails, purge strike.

Mid Rounds

Environment: Reduced field. Remaining targets are more experienced, more paranoid, varying their routines. Pre-game intelligence is becoming stale. Everyone is managing their Splash more carefully.

Strategic directive: Shift toward intelligence-intensive approaches. Purge strikes become more valuable because mandatory exposure cannot be defended away. Social settings become more viable strike opportunities as paranoid targets still maintain their normal social lives.

Update your intelligence: Refresh Splash cadence mapping. The movement profile you built pre-game may have changed significantly.

Late Rounds

Environment: Small field. Purges become more frequent per the rules. Every remaining team is competent and has survived multiple rounds. Your hunter is almost certainly skilled.

Strategic directive: Purge strikes are now your primary offensive weapon. With increased purge frequency, mandatory exposure events happen often enough to anchor your offensive planning around them. Maintain maximum defensive discipline — a late-round elimination from a defensive failure is a catastrophic waste of everything you've built.

Alliance caution: In late rounds, every remaining team is a future opponent. Share nothing sensitive in any alliance negotiation. The mutual target chain is still viable but execute it with the understanding that your ally today is your enemy next round.

SECTION 6 — CONTINGENCY DECISION TREE

For any offensive operation, run through this tree before proceeding.

Q1: Do you know where your target currently is?
No → Check Splash. If unavailable, abort and wait for location data.
Yes → Proceed to Q2.

Q2: Is your hunter currently mobile or near your home?
Yes → Execute bait ping. Delay departure by 10 minutes minimum. Recheck.
No → Proceed to Q3.

Q3: Is your staging position clean? (No signs of target awareness, no unexpected people near their property)
No → Abort. Do not approach. Reassess within 48 hours.
Yes → Proceed to Q4.

Q4: Are both partners in position, communication established, filming ready?
No → Do not execute. Wait until fully staged.
Yes → Execute.

Q5 (Post-execution): Is the hit clean, documented, unambiguous on video?
No → Do not submit. Attempt to re-engage if still in a legal execution zone.
Yes → Exfil immediately. Submit video within the hour.

SECTION 7 — PARTNER ROLES AND COMMUNICATION PROTOCOL

Permanent role assignment: Designate before the game starts and do not rotate mid-operation.

Partner 1 — The Executor: Responsible for the physical hit. Carries the water gun at all times in the field. Makes all real-time approach decisions. Has final call on execute vs abort once staged.

Partner 2 — The Filmmaker/Driver/Overwatch: Responsible for the continuous elimination video. Drives during all operations. Maintains broader situational awareness while Partner 1 is focused on the target. Has authority to call abort if they see a threat Partner 1 hasn't seen.

Communication protocol:

  • All in-field communication via live phone call only. No texting during active operations — response latency is too high.
  • Pre-agreed call words: Green = target visible and moving toward execution zone. Execute = move now. Abort = stand down immediately, do not approach, exit without drawing attention. Compromised = we have been identified, break off and exfil.
  • If communication is lost during an operation, Partner 1's default action is to hold position. Partner 2's default action is to drive to the last known position of Partner 1 and reestablish contact.
